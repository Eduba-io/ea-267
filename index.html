The frame, applied to Salute360

A working digital-health platform is mostly database and workflow work. Some of it is rule-based. A small part is genuinely AI.

~60%

Database and workflow engineering

Agenda, payments, logistics, e-commerce, the televisit plumbing. Postgres, queues, and integrations do this faster, cheaper, and with better guarantees than a model.

~30%

Rule-based logic

Eligibility, prescription checks, HIPAA and GDPR handling, BAA governance, pharmacy availability rules. A rules engine with an audit trail, not an LLM.

~10%

Genuine AI work

Clinical summarization, multilingual HCP search ranking, patient triage chat, cohort targeting for Bayer-style content partnerships. Real candidates, once the other two layers are named.

The orchestration audit sorts which AI features belong in the ten percent and which belong in a rules engine or a database. The output is a short list, not a vendor pitch.

The relevant case study

Closest match: a product-company CTO engagement.

Feeld (the CTO) in 2025. A scoped sprint delivered a workshop, advisory calls, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework. The company left with a decision framework for which of its live AI candidates got built, which got bought, and which got deferred.

For a team of ten running ten modules, that is the shape of the work.

Why this analog

  • Founder-led product decisions, not a vendor RFP
  • Existing stack kept, orchestration layer added on top
  • Six weeks to a decision framework, not a proof of concept
  • AI candidates triaged against rules and database alternatives

The relevant paper

Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM).

Published via ACM TiiS. Folder-structured agent context, L0 through L4, with measurable interpretability gains. Useful when the platform has to present an inspectable architecture to US health-system vendor review. Repository on request.
